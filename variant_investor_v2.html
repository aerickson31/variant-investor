The Opportunity

The fuel supply
constraint

Every LWR-based SMR entering the market — NuScale, BWRX-300, Holtec, Rolls-Royce — requires uranium enriched to 3–5% U-235. That enrichment must be purchased commercially from one of a small number of qualified suppliers. As of 2024, roughly 44% of global enrichment capacity sits in Russia. Western alternatives exist, but capacity is constrained and qualification timelines are long.

We are not claiming enrichment is the only bottleneck. Conversion capacity, qualified fuel fabricators, and regulatory approval of new fuel designs are all real constraints. The point is more specific: enrichment is the constraint with the deepest geopolitical exposure and the longest lead time to replace. At fleet scale — dozens of LWR SMRs operating — it becomes structural.

HALEU (high-assay LEU at 5–20%) is the acute near-term chokepoint for some advanced designs. Standard 3–5% LEU is a lower-grade concern today, but concentration of production capacity in hostile-adjacent jurisdictions is a planning risk that utilities, governments, and sophisticated investors are actively pricing.

VARIANT's design basis eliminates enrichment dependency entirely for baseline operation. This is not a marginal improvement — it is a structurally different position in the fuel cycle. That position becomes more valuable as geopolitical friction increases and as domestic fuel sovereignty becomes an explicit procurement criterion.

What We Are Claiming

A heavy-water moderated SMR with a credible path to NRC licensing, natural uranium fuel operation, and a first-of-kind demo at a DOE facility within five years. Not magic — a specific, fundable technical program with identified advantages and acknowledged risks.

Why VARIANT

Three structural
advantages

01
No Enrichment Required

Heavy-water moderation provides enough neutron economy to sustain criticality on natural uranium at 0.7% U-235. Deuterium absorbs 1/635th of the neutrons hydrogen would capture, preserving enough flux for fuel that LWRs cannot use. This is not a design aspiration — it is the operating basis of every CANDU reactor built since the 1960s. VARIANT inherits that physics and applies it in a new coolant architecture.

Safety Architecture

Three layers,
each passive

VARIANT's safety case rests on three distinct functions, each with a passive mechanism that requires no operator action, no external power, and no active components during the first several hours of any design-basis event. The demo program is specifically structured to validate all three experimentally.

We avoid the phrase "inherent safety" in regulatory contexts. The correct framing for NRC reviewers is: passive shutdown with validated reactivity coefficients, passive decay heat removal with calculated time constants, and a confinement strategy with a bounded source term from low-pressure inert-coolant operation.

1
Reactivity Termination
Dual independent shutdown systems: gravity-drop shutoff rods (primary) and moderator dump to below-grade tank (secondary). Moderator dump is fail-safe by gravity — no power, no pumps. Once the D₂O is drained, the core is deeply subcritical at any fuel loading. Restart requires deliberate re-fill, which is an operational control, not a safety concern. Restart time after dump is hours, which is a grid reliability tradeoff acknowledged in the design.
2
Decay Heat Removal
The D₂O moderator mass acts as a primary heat sink following scram. Natural convection loops carry heat from the calandria to external air coolers with no pumps. Time-to-boil calculations (to be validated by demo) indicate several hours of passive margin before any fuel temperature limit is approached. Secondary passive path via shell-and-tube HX natural draft available. This function is the primary objective of the demo passive heat removal test.
3
Confinement Philosophy
Helium operating at 3–4 MPa (vs. PWR primary at ~15 MPa) dramatically reduces the stored energy driving any loss-of-coolant event. An inert coolant produces no hydrogen, no steam explosion, no chemical reaction with fuel cladding. Confinement rather than a large leak-tight containment is the design basis, pending formal source term analysis. Demo will characterize fission product release under controlled fault conditions to bound the confinement design for the commercial unit.

DOD installations and federal facilities operating in energy-constrained environments require assured, on-site power that cannot be disrupted by grid events or fuel supply interruption. VARIANT's natural uranium basis means the fuel supply chain has zero dependence on foreign enrichment capacity — a direct national security benefit DOD can quantify. At 65–70 MWe, a single unit can serve a mid-size installation. The small footprint, low operating pressure, and passive safety profile simplify siting on existing federal land. The SRS location itself as the demo site creates a direct DOE-to-DOD deployment pathway.

VARIANT fit: Natural U fuel = zero enrichment exposure • Small footprint • Passive safety = siting flexibility • DOE relationship = federal procurement pathway
Primary Market 2
Industrial Process Heat & Cogeneration

At 380°C helium outlet, VARIANT produces steam conditions that every LWR SMR cannot reach. Heavy industry — chemical processing, refining, advanced manufacturing — uses large quantities of medium-pressure steam at 300–400°C. Currently sourced from natural gas boilers. A collocated nuclear cogeneration unit displaces that gas load with zero-carbon heat at locked long-term cost. The buyer is an industrial operator who wants to decarbonize process heat and lock in energy cost — not a utility buying electrons. This buyer has a different economics model (heat value + carbon credit + energy security), a higher willingness to pay, and is less sensitive to grid integration complexity.

VARIANT fit: 380°C outlet beats all LWR SMRs • Small unit = collocated siting • Natural U = no enrichment supply risk for industrial buyer • Cogeneration = 2× revenue per unit

VARIANT at 65–70 MWe does not serve a hyperscale campus alone, but as part of a multi-unit deployment or paired with renewables, it provides the dispatchable baseload that makes an otherwise intermittent portfolio firm. The data center buyer cares about CFE 24/7 certification and long-term price certainty — both VARIANT strengths. This market is speculative for first units but is a realistic target for units 5–10.

Fuel Cycle & Waste

Simple in, simple
out

VARIANT's FOAK and demo operate on a once-through natural uranium fuel cycle. This is the simplest possible fuel strategy and the one we are designing and licensing around. No reprocessing intent. No MOX qualification required for commercial operation. No spent fuel reprocessing assumptions embedded in the economics.

Fuel form: standard UO₂ pellets in CANDU-heritage 37-element bundles. The same fuel geometry operated commercially for 60 years with a well-characterized irradiation database. No new fuel qualification program required for natural uranium operation — only for future fuel types, which are treated as Phase 2 options.

Refueling scheme for FOAK: batch mode on an approximately 18-month cycle. Online refueling capability is designed into the pressure tube architecture from day one — fuel channel end-fittings are compatible with CANDU-style fueling machines — but is not deployed on the FOAK. Online refueling is demonstrated on the second commercial unit. This choice eliminates the single largest licensing complexity from the first plant.

Discharge burnup target: approximately 7–10 GWd/tU (natural uranium, consistent with CANDU operating experience). Spent fuel is discharged to on-site water storage and subsequently to standard dry storage casks. The pathway is identical to existing CANDU spent fuel management practice — no novel waste disposition approach is required or claimed.

On reprocessing: we are not pursuing it. Spent fuel goes to dry storage on the same regulatory pathway as every operating PHWR. This is not ambiguous.
— VARIANT Design Basis, Once-Through Fuel Cycle, Rev. 1

Source Term (Why Lower Than PHWR)
Helium coolant is not D₂O — tritium produced in moderator stays in the calandria system, not the primary heat transport loop. This eliminates the major tritium permeation pathway that governs CANDU primary circuit dose. Tritium inventory is contained in a single, cooled, low-pressure D₂O system.
Production Rate (Demo)
At 10–20 MWth, tritium production is approximately 1–3 grams/year — consistent with CANDU scaling (~57g/year at 200 MWth). Not a significant commercial quantity. Operational monitoring via D₂O sampling on a defined schedule; cover gas tritium monitor continuous.
Detritiation Approach
SRS SRNL operates existing detritiation capability. Demo inventory detritiated prior to transfer. Commercial units: dedicated small-scale Combined Electrolysis and Catalytic Exchange (CECE) unit, proven technology deployed at CANDU stations. Budget: $2–4M capital, ~$1M/year operating.

Tritium effluent from D₂O systems managed under NRC license condition. Worker dose managed via air monitoring, respiratory protection for D₂O maintenance, and established ALARA programs from CANDU practice. No novel regulatory pathway required — DOE SRS has tritium license experience.

Access to tritiated SRS inventory via DOE transfer or lease, plus SRNL detritiation services, reduces moderator capital cost by 85–90% versus open-market D₂O purchase. Quantified, not estimated.

Demo Program

What the demo
de-risks

The demo is a 10–20 MWth non-power research reactor at SRS, licensed under 10 CFR Part 50 Subpart D — the same framework as the Georgia Tech GTRR and the currently operating NIST NBSR. It does not generate commercial electricity. Its sole purpose is to produce a validated technical data package that supports the commercial FOAK license application and the subsequent $800M–1B financing round.

RELLIS (Texas A&M) runs in parallel as the non-nuclear test platform. An electrically-heated He/D₂O channel mockup — no fissile material, no NRC permit — generates thermal-hydraulic correlation data that feeds into the SRS license application. RELLIS hardware running in 18–24 months is our most important near-term investor-visible milestone.

The demo retires risk in investor terms: it converts technical uncertainty into measured data. Each item below corresponds to a specific uncertainty that today requires assumptions and that, after the demo, becomes a measurement.

R1
Neutronics Validation
Low-power physics measurements (critical approach, flux mapping, reactivity coefficient measurements) validate our neutronics codes against actual D₂O/helium-channel core geometry. Bias and uncertainty quantification feeds directly into the commercial FOAK license safety analysis. Without measured data, regulators require large conservative margins. With it, margins are defensible and tighter.
Retires: Code validation uncertainty for criticality safety analysis
R2
Thermal-Hydraulic Correlations
Helium channel flow, heat transfer coefficients, and pressure drop in the actual fuel channel geometry at operating conditions. RELLIS provides first-cut data; the demo provides nuclear-environment confirmation. The D₂O/helium interface behavior — particularly any cross-contamination or permeation under operating conditions — is measured and bounded. This data does not exist in published literature for this specific configuration.
Retires: TH correlation uncertainty and interface behavior assumptions
R3
Passive Decay Heat Removal Validation
Controlled scram followed by disconnection of active cooling. Temperature coast-down is measured and compared to pre-test predictions. Time to reach any defined temperature limit is established experimentally. This is the single most important safety data point for the commercial license application and the most fundable result the demo produces.
Retires: Passive safety claim uncertainty — converts calculation to measurement
R4
Fuel Channel Mechanical Behavior
Pressure tube performance under helium coolant at operating temperature: creep rate, dimensional stability, end-fitting helium leak rate, vibration characteristics. Even 6–12 months of irradiation provides the FOAK license application with measured data on the most novel mechanical component in the design.
Retires: Pressure tube qualification uncertainty at VARIANT operating conditions
R5
Operations & Instrumentation Behavior
Startup, load change, and shutdown procedures against actual plant response. Instrumentation calibration and drift behavior in the radiation environment. Abnormal transient response vs. simulation predictions. Human factors data for the commercial FOAK operating procedures and training program basis.
Retires: Operational procedure uncertainty and instrumentation qualification gaps
